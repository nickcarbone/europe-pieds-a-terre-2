GE · TBILISI

Tbilisi

Vera · Vake

Dropped from round 1: Georgia is not a typical "European city" frame — but strategically it may be the most important entry in both files combined.

Wildcard Not Schengen. Doesn't cost a single day of your 90-day allowance.
The Schengen pressure valve

Every Schengen property you own competes for the same 90 days per rolling 180-day window. Tbilisi is the only city in either file that costs you nothing against that clock — Georgia offers US citizens up to one year visa-free on entry. That means you can base yourself here for months at a time while renting out your Schengen properties, then make a Schengen circuit when the calendar allows. At $100k–$200k for a well-finished apartment in Vera or Vake, it also frees capital for a better unit elsewhere. Rental income taxed at a flat 5%. Zero capital gains after two years of ownership.

Vera and Vake are Tbilisi's most established upscale and expat-heavy neighborhoods — Vake is anchored by a large park, international schools, and a 5–6% gross rental yield profile; Vera sits closer to Rustaveli Avenue with boutique-hotel demand and Airbnb yields approaching 10% on smaller units. Foreigners buy with the same rights as Georgian citizens, legal fees rarely exceed $1,000, and property registration can cost as little as $20. The construction standards require careful due diligence — "black frame," "white frame," and "green frame" completions vary wildly in finish quality — but a new premium building in Vera at $2,000/m² produces a flat well inside your budget. The market hit record 40,350 transactions in 2024. New-build average: $1,312/m². Chugureti, adjacent to the Old Town, saw 28.3% price growth in Q1 2025 alone.

EE · TALLINN

Tallinn

Kalamaja

Dropped from round 1: Vilnius and Riga were already filling the Baltic slot. Kalamaja is the stronger lifestyle pick; Riga has the stronger aesthetic argument.

Open No apartment quota for foreigners; remote e-notary purchase possible

Kalamaja is Tallinn's Ruzafa: a wooden-house industrial-converted creative district that has drawn expats, digital nomads, and young professionals for a decade, now with Telliskivi Creative City at its center and a tram upgrade (Pelguranna line, design phase now, completion ~2029) that should push values 5–15%. Apartments in renovated wooden buildings run €220,000–€350,000 — sitting cleanly inside budget. Estonia imposes no foreign ownership quota on apartments, closing costs are among the lowest in the EU (0.8–1.8%), and the e-notary system means you can close a deal without traveling to Tallinn. Annual land tax is €20–€120 on a typical apartment. STR is active and unlicensed in any ban zone in Kalamaja.

BE · GHENT

Ghent

Patershol · Prinsenhof · Citadelpark fringe

Dropped from round 1: Belgium's resale registration tax (10–12.5% in Flanders) is a genuine hit on a €300k purchase — you're starting ~€36,000 in the hole versus Italy's ~3% transfer tax.

Watch No STR ban; budget-of-year VAT registration required for hosts

Ghent is the most underrated city in northwest Europe for this brief — medieval canal quarter, car-free center, Flemish Gothic architecture, Gravensteen castle, 35 minutes from Brussels by train, and a genuinely residential rather than tourist-saturated character compared to Bruges. Patershol is the charming cobblestone quarter for character; the Citadelpark fringe near Sint-Pieters station is calmer and leafier. Belgium's Flemish Region requires a VAT "budget of the year" registration for short-term hosts who cross a commercial threshold, but no city-level ban exists in Ghent. The fatal math: Flanders charges 12.5% droits d'enregistrement on resale properties. New-build VAT is 21%, which is worse. This is the reason it fell off — not lifestyle, which scores very well.

GR · THESSALONIKI

Thessaloniki

Kalamaria (not Ano Poli or the center)

Dropped from round 1: Athens was already in the file; Greece has limited the sense of adding a second Greek city. Then on June 24, 2026, the First Municipal Community STR freeze was extended.

Watch New STR freeze in First Municipal Community from Jul 1, 2026; Kalamaria is outside it

As of July 1, 2026, Thessaloniki's historic and commercial core — the Aristotelous Square / Egnatia axis and surrounding neighborhoods — joined Athens under a short-term rental registration freeze, with fines starting at 20,000€ minimum for violations. Kalamaria, the affluent southeastern coastal suburb, is a separate municipality and sits outside this freeze. It's Thessaloniki's most self-contained "leafy and elite" option: waterfront promenade, metro-connected, well-maintained, family-oriented, with 2-3BR apartments running €250,000–€400,000. Pricing per m²: renovated units at €2,400–€3,200, new builds approaching €3,500+ in seafront positions. Rental yields run 3.0–4.2% gross for long-term family lets, higher for medium-term.

IT · NAPLES

Naples

Chiaia · Posillipo

Dropped from round 1: Italy already had Bologna. The citizenship lawsuit is filed in Naples, which creates an unusual strategic overlap worth noting.

Open National CIN regime applies, same as Bologna — no city-level STR ban

The case filed in Naples means you'll likely have legitimate, recurring travel there over the next 1–3 years, independent of tourism days. Chiaia is the most "calm, leafy, elite" reading of Naples: the waterfront Lungomare, Piazza dei Martiri, tree-lined residential streets running up toward the Posillipo hill, and a social scene anchored by established Neapolitan families rather than backpackers. It's a genuine shock how much better value Naples is than Bologna at comparable quality: a renovated 2BR in Chiaia runs roughly €250,000–€350,000 where a comparable unit in central Bologna would be €320,000–€380,000. Same Italian CIN (codice identificativo nazionale) registration, same 21% flat-rate cedolare secca tax on first-property STR income.

FR · LYON

Lyon

Croix-Rousse

Dropped from round 1: France has one of the EU's highest property purchase tax burdens for non-residents, and proposed new taxes on non-resident owners are moving through legislation.

Caution 7.5% purchase tax + non-resident surcharge proposals; STR permitted but tightening

Croix-Rousse is one of the best neighborhoods in Europe by the criteria in this brief: a genuine village-like hilltop quarter above Lyon's Presqu'île peninsula, with traboules (hidden passageways), a weekly market, independent cafés, and a creative community of silk workers' descendants turned artists. Lyon is a 2-hour TGV from Paris and a hub city with genuinely excellent food culture. The obstacle is France's tax architecture for non-residents: a 7.5% droits de mutation on most resale purchases, capital gains taxed at 36.2% for non-residents vs. 17.2% for French residents, and proposals (introduced in 2025, moving slowly) for additional surcharges on non-resident secondary property owners. STR in Lyon's Presqu'île peninsula has tightened; Croix-Rousse sits above the most affected zones. Worth revisiting if France's non-resident tax framework crystallizes more favorably or if other options run out of steam.

PL · GDAŃSK

Gdańsk

Wrzeszcz · Oliwa

Dropped from round 1: Kraków already filled the Poland slot. Gdańsk is the second Polish pick — less romantic to the tourist eye but stronger on the "leafy and well-connected" criteria.

Open Apartment purchase — same non-EU permit exemption as Kraków; no STR ban

Gdańsk is a Hanseatic city on the Baltic: extraordinary brick Gothic architecture in the Główne Miasto, but the neighborhoods worth living in are Wrzeszcz (tram-connected, tree-lined avenues, the main commercial street Wrzeszcz runs under a canopy of mature linden trees) and Oliwa (quieter, a cathedral and park, next to the Gdańsk Zoo). It's a genuinely livable city with a strong short-term tourist market driven by Poland's Baltic coast summer season, giving STR yields a seasonal dimension that Kraków's year-round market doesn't have. Prices are notably lower than Kraków's historic center — a renovated 2BR in Wrzeszcz runs roughly €180,000–€280,000. Same 2% PCC transfer tax on resale as elsewhere in Poland. No foreign apartment permit required.

HR · SPLIT

Split

Meje · Manuš

Dropped from round 1: non-EU buyers need Ministry of Justice approval (1–6 months) and the reciprocity requirement adds process risk. The moment citizenship resolves, the friction disappears entirely.

Caution Non-EU buyer requires Ministry of Justice approval + reciprocity confirmation; up to 6 months

Croatia's Dalmatian coast is one of Europe's strongest short-term rental markets — Adriatic summer tourist demand drives STR occupancy rates that other cities can only dream of. Split itself is a living city (250,000 residents) rather than just a tourist stage set, and Meje — the calm, elegant western residential peninsula — is about as "leafy and elite" as any Mediterranean neighborhood on either list: villa-lined streets, proximity to the Kaštelet and Meštrovićeva Galerija, and a 10-minute walk along the water to Diocletian's Palace. Non-EU buyers must apply through the Ministry of Interior and wait 1–6 months for approval, referencing the US-Croatia reciprocity agreement. No coastal zone land can be bought by anyone (this is a hard restriction), but apartments within the urban fabric of Split are purchasable — you just need the approval first. 3% property transfer tax at closing.
